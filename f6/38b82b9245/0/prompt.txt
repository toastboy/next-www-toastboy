Options in a private company that doesn't exit before retirement are illiquid and worthless at the moment they're most needed. The peer reviewers were fairly united in calling out the Expansionist as the weakest voice in the room on this point.

The honest synthesis: equity is *potentially* the most important variable, but only under a narrow set of conditions — Series B or later funding, a credible 3-5 year exit horizon, and grants sized meaningfully for the role. If those conditions are met, the Expansionist is right. If the equity answer is vague, deferred, or small, it's noise. The interview will answer this. Don't decide before you have the number.

## Blind Spots the Council Caught

**The health insurance gap is not just a cost — it's an asymmetric risk.**

Three of the five peer reviewers flagged this independently, which makes it the council's clearest shared finding. Not one of the five advisors treated health insurance as anything other than a line-item expense. It isn't. A mid-50s professional who develops a health condition after joining the new company could become uninsurable or face dramatically higher renewal premiums with no employer backstop, precisely at the age when health risk rises. The downside scenario isn't "I pay more for insurance" — it's "I lose continuous coverage and cannot replace it affordably." If there is any pre-existing condition or family health complexity in the picture, this transforms from arithmetic into a hard constraint that potentially vetoes the move regardless of equity upside.

**The pension vesting question was missed entirely.**

Two peer reviewers raised this and none of the advisors addressed it. Four years from a 60-target retirement, the state of the current employer's pension arrangements could dwarf every other variable in this decision. Specifically: is the current scheme defined benefit or defined contribution? Has employer vesting on those contributions been running long enough that leaving now forfeits a tranche? Does moving reset a vesting clock? This single question could make the financial comparison trivially obvious in one direction — and it hasn't been asked.

**The psychological/identity dimension went unexamined.**

One peer reviewer named this directly and it's correct. This decision isn't only financial — it's about how this person wants to spend the last four working years of his career. The council spent almost all its energy on compensation arithmetic and equity scenarios. Nobody asked whether the interview process itself has been energising or draining; whether he has the appetite to prove himself again in a new culture; whether being the most senior technical person in a scale-up at 56 is appealing or quietly exhausting. These questions don't override the financial ones, but they belong in the frame.

## The Recommendation

Go to the final interview. Make it an interrogation, not a performance.

The interview is not the decision point — it is the data-collection mechanism. But the data you collect there will almost certainly resolve the decision cleanly. Here is what you need to leave with:

**Equity:** Get the actual number, vesting schedule, cliff, and what happens on acquisition. If they still won't answer this after three rounds, you have your answer — and it's no.

**Benefits gap:** Before you walk in, get an actual quote for private health insurance at your age and health status, not an estimate. Add the pension contribution delta. Calculate the real all-in compensation difference. If it's 10-15% worse and the equity answer is vague, the decision is made.

**Exit horizon:** Ask directly. Series B or later? Board composition? Revenue growth trajectory? Investor push for liquidity? A 4-year vesting schedule with no exit in sight is worthless at 60.

**Stability:** How long has the current tech leadership been there? What's the funding runway?

If you leave that interview with a clear equity answer, a real exit timeline, and numbers that show a genuine financial upside — meaning the equity is sized meaningfully, vesting aligns with your retirement window, and the all-in compensation holds up — then proceed seriously. The manufacturing company will still be there as a fallback, and the upside is real.

If the equity answer is still deferred, small, or conditional, or if the all-in compensation is materially worse than your current package, decline politely. The stable employer with high retention four years from retirement is not a consolation — it is an objectively good outcome that the framing of this decision has been quietly undervaluing.

One final thing the council kept circling without naming directly: you described your current employer as a good place to "see out your career" in a tone that suggested faint embarrassment. That framing is worth examining before the interview, not after. If the driving force here is not financial but a feeling of being professionally undervalued or intellectually under-stimulated, a final-round FinTech interview is not the right treatment. Name the actual problem first — then decide whether this job solves it.
